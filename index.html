Before you arrive

✈
Visit Japan Web — register before flying
Complete immigration/customs forms online. Generates a QR code that lets you skip paper forms and use the fast lane at immigration. Saves 45–60 mins at Narita or Haneda.
FREE
💳
Suica / Pasmo IC Card
Get at any major airport JR machine or 7-Eleven ATM. Load ¥5,000 to start. Works on all trains, subways, and buses nationwide, plus convenience stores and vending machines.
¥500 deposit
📶
Pocket WiFi or SIM
Order pocket WiFi from IIJmio or Sakura Mobile before departure. Alternatively, buy an eSIM via Ubigi or Airalo before flying. Download offline Google Maps for Tokyo, Kyoto, and Osaka in advance.
¥800–1,200/day
🚄
JR Pass — buy before leaving home
21-day or 30-day JR Pass covers all Shinkansen (except Nozomi/Mizuho), local JR lines, and some buses/ferries. Cannot be purchased in Japan at the lower overseas price. For 30 days covering Tokyo→Hiroshima→Kanazawa→Matsumoto, the pass pays for itself massively.
~¥100,000 (30-day)

Airport to city

🚆
Narita → Tokyo (Narita Express / N'EX)
60 mins to Shinjuku. Buy the N'EX Tokyo Round Trip ticket (¥4,070) — valid for 14 days return. Departs every 30 mins. Better than the Limousine Bus.
¥3,070 one way
🚆
Haneda → Tokyo (Keikyu Line)
13 mins to Shinagawa, then JR to your hotel area. The fastest and cheapest option from Haneda. Runs 24 hours on weekends.
¥410
🧳
Luggage forwarding (Takkyubin / クロネコヤマト)
Japan's single best travel hack. Send bags from airport to hotel on Day 1. Between cities, drop bags at 7-Eleven or hotel front desk by 12pm — arrives at next hotel by 2pm next day. Travel completely hands-free on trains.
¥1,500–2,000/bag

Eating Etiquette Rules

Slurping noodlesCompletely acceptable and even appreciated — shows enjoyment. Slurp your ramen and soba loudly.
No tippingTipping is considered rude in Japan. Never tip at restaurants, taxis, or hotels.
Chopstick rulesNever stand chopsticks upright in rice (funeral omen). Never pass food chopstick to chopstick.
Eating while walkingNot widely acceptable outside festivals and designated market zones. Step aside and eat standing still.

Crowd avoidance

Fushimi Inari: Arrive at 6am sharp. The iconic tunnel path is empty. By 9am it's packed solid.
Arashiyama bamboo: 7am visit. Most influencers show up at 10am. Sunrise light is better anyway.
Shibuya crossing: Best shot from Starbucks 2nd floor window or Mag's Park roof of Shibuya 109.
TeamLab Borderless: Book online 2–3 months ahead. Sold out constantly. No walk-ins.
Nikko: Weekday visit only. Weekend crowds are brutal.

Money hacks

7-Eleven ATM: Accepts all foreign cards, best exchange rate, no surprise fees.
100-yen stores: Daiso and Seria have everything — travel adapters, containers, snacks, stationery.
Konbini meals: Breakfast at 7-Eleven daily saves ¥800–1,200 vs cafes.
Day passes: Kyoto 1-day bus pass (¥700) is worth it if doing Arashiyama + Fushimi same day.
Suica auto-reload: Link a foreign Visa/Mastercard via the Suica app to avoid running out.

Luggage forwarding

Service name: Takkyubin (TA-KYU-BIN) by Yamato Transport. Also called "courier service" at hotels.
Where to drop: Hotel front desk (by 12pm) or any 7-Eleven, Lawson, or FamilyMart.
Cost: ¥1,500–2,000 per bag depending on size.
Delivery timing: Same-day delivery in some areas. Next-day delivery standard.

Phone & navigation

Google Maps: Download offline maps for Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, Hiroshima before departing.
HyperDia: Best app for train route planning including exact platform numbers.
Google Translate camera: Point at menus — it reads kanji in real time. Works offline.
TabiEats / Gurunavi: Restaurant discovery with English menus.
🗑 The trash bag rule: Japan has almost no public bins since 1995. Carry a small ziplock bag as your personal rubbish bag. Konbini have bins near the entrance and accept your trash when you buy something. This is the #1 thing that separates tourist from thoughtful traveler.

Train etiquette

No loud phone calls. Silent mode required; speaking on phone is prohibited.
Priority seats — let elderly, pregnant, and disabled passengers have them always.
Queue in the marked lines on the platform. Follow them precisely.
No eating on local/metro trains (bullet train dining cars are fine).
Backpacks go on the rack above or in front of you in crowded cars.

Temple & shrine rules

Torii gates: Walk on the side, not down the center — the center lane is for the gods.
Temizuya: Rinse left hand, right hand, then left hand holding water, then the ladle handle.
Shrine approach: Bow twice, clap twice, pray, bow once more.
Photography: Inner sanctuaries are often no-photo zones. Heed the signs.
Shoes: Remove shoes before entering tatami rooms and traditional restaurants.

Public behavior

Blowing your nose in public is considered rude. Sniff discreetly or find a private space.
Eating or drinking while walking is frowned upon except at markets and festivals.
Pointing is rude — use your whole open hand to gesture toward something.
Stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo (right in Osaka).
Never hand money directly person-to-person — place it on the small tray provided.

Onsen rules

Tattoos: Most onsen ban visible tattoos. Inquire at private bath onsens which are tattoo-friendly.
Shower first: Always wash your body completely before entering the communal bath.
No swimwear: Traditional onsen are nude. It is not optional.
Small towel: Keep it on your head or at your side — never in the water.
After onsen: Hydrate immediately. Don't shower after — the minerals are beneficial.
