Tips & hacks

Before you arrive

✈
Visit Japan Web — register before flying
Complete immigration/customs forms online at vjw-lp.digital.go.jp. Generates a QR code that lets you skip the paper forms and use the fast lane at immigration. Saves 45–60 mins at Narita or Haneda.
FREE
💳
Suica / Pasmo IC Card
Get at any major airport JR machine or 7-Eleven ATM. Load ¥5,000 to start. Works on all trains, subways, and buses nationwide, plus 7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart, and most vending machines. No need to buy tickets at machines ever again.
¥500 deposit
📶
Pocket WiFi or SIM
Order pocket WiFi from IIJmio or Sakura Mobile before departure — pick it up at the airport arrivals hall. Alternatively, buy an eSIM (Ubigi / Airalo) before flying. Google Maps works offline if you download Tokyo / Kyoto / Osaka maps in advance.
¥800–1,200/day
🚄
JR Pass — buy before leaving home
21-day or 30-day JR Pass covers ALL Shinkansen (except Nozomi/Mizuho), local JR lines, and some buses/ferries. Cannot be purchased in Japan at the lower overseas price. For 30 days covering Tokyo→Hiroshima→Kanazawa→Matsumoto, the pass pays for itself massively.
~¥100,000 (30-day)

Airport to city

🚆
Narita → Tokyo (Narita Express / N'EX)
60 mins to Shinjuku. Buy the N'EX Tokyo Round Trip ticket (¥4,070) — valid for 14 days return. Departs every 30 mins. Much better than the Limousine Bus (90+ mins, traffic risk).
¥3,070 one way
🚆
Haneda → Tokyo (Keikyu Line)
13 mins to Shinagawa, then JR to your hotel area. The fastest and cheapest option from Haneda. Runs 24 hours on weekends.
¥410
🧳
Luggage forwarding (Takkyubin / クロネコヤマト)
Japan's single best travel hack. Send bags from airport to hotel on Day 1 — arrives same evening or next morning. Between cities, drop bags at 7-Eleven or hotel front desk by 12pm and they arrive at next hotel by 2pm next day. ¥1,500–2,000 per bag. Travel completely hands-free on trains.
¥1,500–2,000/bag

Eating etiquette rules

Slurping noodlesCompletely acceptable and even appreciated — shows enjoyment. Slurp your ramen and soba loudly.
No tippingTipping is considered rude in Japan. Never tip at restaurants, taxis, hotels, or anywhere. Service is included in the pride of work.
Oshibori (wet towel)Use to clean hands only — not face. Fold neatly after use and place on the side of your tray or table.
Chopstick rulesNever stand chopsticks upright in rice (funeral omen). Never pass food chopstick to chopstick (another funeral custom).
Eating while walkingNot widely acceptable outside festivals and designated market zones. If you get street food at Nishiki, step aside and eat standing still.

Crowd avoidance

Fushimi Inari: Arrive at 6am sharp. The iconic tunnel path is empty. By 9am it's packed solid.
Arashiyama bamboo: 7am visit. Most influencers show up at 10am. Sunrise light is better anyway.
Shibuya crossing: Best shot from Starbucks 2nd floor window or Mag's Park on the roof of Shibuya 109.
TeamLab Borderless: Book online 2–3 months ahead. Sold out constantly. No walk-ins available.
Nikko: Weekday visit only. Weekend crowds are brutal. Day 3 (Wednesday) in this itinerary is ideal timing.

Money hacks

7-Eleven ATM: Accepts all foreign cards, best exchange rate, no surprise fees. Japan Post Bank ATMs are second best.
100-yen stores: Daiso and Seria have everything — travel adapters, containers, snacks, stationery. Better than airport prices.
Konbini meals: Breakfast at 7-Eleven daily saves ¥800–1,200 vs cafes. Use this budget for one extra experience.
Day passes: Kyoto 1-day bus pass (¥700) is worth it if doing Arashiyama + Fushimi in same day.
Suica auto-reload: Link a foreign Visa/Mastercard via the Suica app to avoid running out of balance.

Luggage forwarding

Service name: Takkyubin (TA-KYU-BIN) by Yamato Transport. Also called "courier service" at hotels.
Where to drop: Hotel front desk (by 12pm) or any 7-Eleven, Lawson, or FamilyMart.
Cost: ¥1,500–2,000 per bag depending on size. Box/oversized charges extra.
Delivery timing: Same-day delivery possible in some areas. Next-day delivery standard. Specify check-in date.
Envelope trick: At hotels, fill out the slip the night before. Morning Shinkansen departure becomes effortless.

Phone & navigation

Google Maps: Download offline maps for Tokyo, Kyoto, Osaka, Hiroshima before departing.
HyperDia: Best app for train route planning including exact platform numbers and transfers.
Google Translate camera: Point at menus — it reads kanji in real time. Works offline once Japanese is downloaded.
TabiEats / Gurunavi: Restaurant discovery with English menus. Filter by area and cuisine type.
Japan Official Travel App: Free WiFi finder, disaster alerts, and tourist info from JNTO.
🗑 The trash bag rule: Japan has almost no public bins since 1995 (Sarin attack aftermath). Carry a small ziplock bag as your personal rubbish bag. Konbini (convenience stores) have bins near the entrance and accept your trash when you buy something. This is the #1 thing that separates tourist from thoughtful traveler.
🩺 Medical hack: Bring your own basic medications — paracetamol (Tylenol equivalent), antihistamines, and upset-stomach pills from home. Japanese pharmacies (Matsumoto Kiyoshi) are excellent but foreign brands differ. Japan's own Seirogan works well for digestion. If you need prescription medicine, bring a translated letter from your doctor plus extra supply.

Train etiquette

No loud phone calls. Silent mode is required; speaking on phone is prohibited.
Priority seats (silver/blue markings) — stand near them but let elderly, pregnant, and disabled passengers have them always.
Queue in the marked lines on the platform. The lines are for specific train doors — follow them precisely.
No eating on local/metro trains (bullet train dining cars are fine).
Backpacks go on the rack above or in front of you — not on your back in crowded cars.

Temple & shrine rules

Torii gates: Walk on the side, not down the center — the center lane is for the gods.
Temizuya (water purification): Rinse left hand, right hand, then left hand holding water, then the ladle handle.
Shrine approach: Bow twice, clap twice, pray, bow once more. Called ni-rei ni-hakushu ichi-rei.
Photography: Inner sanctuaries are often no-photo zones. Heed the signs. Asking before photographing monks is respectful.
Shoes: Remove shoes before entering tatami rooms, traditional restaurants, and many shrines. Slip-on shoes recommended.

Public behavior

Blowing your nose in public is considered rude. Sniff discreetly or find a private space.
Eating or drinking while walking is frowned upon except at markets and festivals.
Pointing is rude — use your whole open hand to gesture toward something.
Stand on the left side of escalators in Tokyo (right in Osaka) to let people pass on the active side.
Never hand money directly person-to-person at shops — place it on the small tray provided.

Onsen rules

Tattoos: Most onsen ban visible tattoos. Cover-up stickers available at some. Inquire at private bath onsens (kashikiri-buro) which are tattoo-friendly.
Shower first: Always wash your body completely at the shower stations before entering the communal bath.
No swimwear: Traditional onsen are nude. It is not optional. Some newer onsen allow swimwear — check beforehand.
Small towel: The small towel is for modesty while walking. Keep it on your head or at your side — never in the water.
After onsen: Hydrate immediately. Drink water at the vending machine just outside. Don't shower after — the minerals are beneficial.
